Sarah Klein

Sarah Klein is an upright and electric bass player, and has also been known to dabble on the piano, guitar, ukulele, and mandolin. She currently plays with the Dirty Mother String Band, the New York Repertory Orchestra, and in any number of community theater pits, jazz and bluegrass jams, and cozy living rooms around Manhattan and Brooklyn. She teaches upright bass and music theory from her sunny music room.

Sarah has been fortunate to play at a number of special and historic venues, including Benaroya Hall and the Columbia Center in Seattle, Sala Multiusos in Zaragoza, and the American Victory Ship in Tampa.

She studied Music Theory at the University of Florida under Kevin Casseday, where she performed in the Symphony Orchestra, Wind Symphony, fall musicals, and anywhere else with a need for some low notes. She also performed with the Ocala Symphony Orchestra and could be found at many brunches and bars with The Tramp Collection.

After Gainesville, Sarah moved to Seattle, WA where she performed with the Seattle Philharmonic Orchestra and the opera/bolero ensemble Encanto.

Fond Memories

Sarah is an enthusiastic amateur photographer. She has taken photos for ChickTech, Sofar Sounds, and countless headshots, gigs, and graduations. As evident below, she also enjoys travel photography.

Sarah Klein has been writing code since before she could legally drive (though, admittedly, not very good code).

She obtained a B.A. in Digital Arts and Sciences Engineering at the University of Florida. During her 4+1 years of undergrad, she completed 3 technical internships at Microsoft, two in Seattle, WA and one in Mountain View, CA. She also had a delightful stint at Grooveshark before they, unfortunately, had to shut their doors.

In addition to her technical internships, Sarah represented DAS on the Engineering Student Advisory Council, participated in the Undergraduate Scholars Program, and published Point/Counterpoint - Verifying Two-Voice Species Counterpoint in the Style of Palestrina, based on a semester spent developing a web application that would help undergraduate students learn basic counterpoint (and thereby saving their professors quite a few red pens).

After university and a cross-country road trip, Sarah spent two years at Microsoft, where she played with bluetooth technologies, did some soldering, dove into data, wrote firmware, and began an exploration of Computer Vision.

After cutting her teeth at Microsoft, Sarah started at the New York Times as a web developer, but soon became a Go engineer with a focus on personalization. Her projects have included algorithmically optimizing the programming of the homepage's Science & Technology block, creating NYT's first personalized email, and quite a few punny internal tools.

Sarah is an avid knitter, an occasional crocheter, and a reluctant seamstress. You can find her self-published knitting patterns on Ravelry, or you can see her other creations on Instagram. Feel free to reach out for test-knitting, sample-making, or custom commissions - she loves a project!
